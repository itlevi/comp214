4.1 Review an Article or Lecture on HCI

Storyboarding is a crucial step in the human-computer interaction design process and is often overlooked. The storyboard and prototypes should start off very low fidelity and increase in detail over the course of the project. Once the storyboarding has solidified the flow and idea of a project, the creation of paper prototypes can begin. Digital mock-ups should be the last step in the planning phase, and only done when all stakeholders have agreed upon some design concepts.

The main focus of the storyboard should be on communicating the flow and idea while attempting to illustrate the goal at a high level. The drawings are intended to be low fidelity, so drawing skills are unnecessary, and spending extra time trying to make the images pretty in this phase is often time wasted. A successful storyboard will convey the task being accomplished, the sequence of the steps involved to accomplish said task and communicating the need that is being fulfilled by completing the task. Since storyboarding is in the early stages of design, there is little time lost if an idea needs to be scrapped.

The lecturer Scott Klemmer emphasizes the need to multiple mock-ups during the design process. Not only should it be in the initial phase, but it should also be an iterative task throughout the design process.

Digital mock-ups can begin once the important groundwork has been completed and all stakeholders have agreed upon the higher-level idea of the interface.

Glossary:
Storyboard: a sequence of drawings, typically with some directions and dialogue, representing the shots planned for a movie or television production.
Mock-up: a model or replica of a machine or structure, used for instructional or experimental purposes.
